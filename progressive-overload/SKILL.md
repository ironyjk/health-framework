# Progressive Overload

> **Background and theory**: Read [references/foundation.md](references/foundation.md)


## One-Line Summary

**The body adapts only when the stimulus continuously exceeds what came before.** Repeating the same stimulus yields nothing beyond maintenance. Load is not the only variable — overload is the combination of five variables (load, reps, sets, frequency, range, density).


## When to Use It

- **The foundation of every resistance training program**
- **Plateau diagnosis** — "Have the load, volume, or frequency of this exercise moved in the last 4 weeks?"
- **Beginner program design** — linear progression
- **Intermediate/advanced reassessment** — consciously choose which variable you're overloading


## Practical Application

### Beginner Linear (Starting Strength style)
```
3x/week full body
- Squat 3x5: +2.5kg every session
- Bench/Overhead alternating 3x5: +2.5kg
- Deadlift 1x5: +5kg
2–3 failed attempts → reduce 10% and retry (re-loading)
```

### Intermediate Weekly Overload
```
Week 1: 5x5 @70%
Week 2: 5x5 @72.5%
Week 3: 5x5 @75%
Week 4: deload 3x5 @60%
Or Wendler 5/3/1: +2.5/5kg training max on a monthly cycle
```

### Advanced Volume Block (RP-style)
```
MEV → add sets per muscle group each week → reach MRV → deload → new block
```

### Variable-Selection Decision Tree
```
Is your 1RM or top set going up?
├─ Yes → keep progressing load
└─ No → check technique and recovery
    ├─ Technical flaw → hold volume, relearn technique
    ├─ Insufficient recovery → move to recovery-periodization
    ├─ Pain or injury signals → substitute exercise + medical evaluation (see strength-basics substitution table)
    └─ Intermediate/advanced → prioritize volume and frequency progression
```

### Autoregulation — RPE/RIR Based

On days when linear progression is impractical (sleep deprivation, stress, post-illness), modulate stimulus intensity rather than clinging to fixed numbers.

- **RPE 10 = failure, 9 = 1 rep in reserve, 8 = 2 reps, 7 = 3 reps**
- Plan: "Squat 4x5 @RPE 8." Allow ±5–10% load variation based on that day's readiness
- **Intra-week ramp**: RPE 7 → 8 → 9 increasing intensity. Next week, progress volume or load
- **Top set + back-off**: record 1 set @RPE 9 → 2–3 back-off sets at -10% @RPE 7
- RPE judgment is inaccurate during the first 3–6 months. Reference Tuchscherer RTS and Helms discussions


## Measurement

- **A training log is mandatory** (date, exercise, load, reps, RPE, sets)
- Track RPE or RIR (Reps in Reserve)
- Self-review every 3–4 weeks: "What went up compared to the prior period?"


## Anti-Patterns

- **Attempting a max every session** — exceeds recovery, raises injury risk, collapses technique
- **Running the same program unchanged for 6+ months**
- **Believing load is the only form of progression** — especially wrong for advanced lifters
- **Confusing conditioning/cardio with resistance overload** — different goals
- **Ego lifting** — shortening range of motion to push the number = fake overload
- **Skipping deloads** — "I'll get weaker" is the most common failure mode
- **Progressing too aggressively** — the nervous system adapts quickly, connective tissue slowly. Tendon and joint injuries are common
- **Testing 1RM too often** — erodes weekly training adaptation. Space tests 4–12 weeks apart


## Limitations

1. **Infinite progression is impossible** — genetics, age, and training age impose ceilings
2. **Progression is not the goal itself** — the stimulus appropriate to your goal (hypertrophy, strength, health) comes first
3. **Paradoxical during injury** — linear overload can itself be the driver of injury
4. **Requires accurate measurement** — without records, you're judging overload subjectively → misjudgment
5. **Individual differences in recovery** — the same volume is MEV for one person and MRV for another
